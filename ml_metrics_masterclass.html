Accuracy / Precision / Recall / Specificity / F1

OK
"Five lenses on the same confusion matrix — each tells a different part of the story."
Formulas
\(\text{Accuracy} = \frac{TP + TN}{TP + TN + FP + FN}\)
\(\text{Precision} = \frac{TP}{TP + FP}\)
\(\text{Recall} = \frac{TP}{TP + FN}\)
\(\text{Specificity} = \frac{TN}{TN + FP}\)
\(F_1 = \frac{2 \cdot P \cdot R}{P + R}\)
Layman Analogy
Accuracy = how often you're right overall. Precision = when you say yes, how often are you actually right? Recall = of all actual positives, how many did you catch? Specificity = of all actual negatives, how many did you correctly rule out? F1 = the harmonic balance between precision and recall.
Red Flag If accuracy is high but precision or recall is low, you may have class imbalance masking poor performance.
Pro Tip In fraud detection, recall matters more — missing a fraud is worse than a false alarm.

Balanced Accuracy

OK
"Regular accuracy lies when classes are imbalanced. Balanced accuracy gives each class equal weight."
Formula
\(\text{Balanced Accuracy} = \frac{\text{Sensitivity} + \text{Specificity}}{2}\)
Layman Analogy
Like grading a test where each topic counts equally, regardless of how many questions it had. If your model aces the majority class but flunks the minority, balanced accuracy will expose that.
Why It Matters
With 95% negatives, always predicting "negative" gives 95% accuracy but 50% balanced accuracy — revealing the model is useless.

—
Red Flag Balanced accuracy below 0.6 signals the model is struggling on at least one class. Below 0.5 means worse than random.
Pro Tip Always report balanced accuracy alongside standard accuracy when presenting to stakeholders on imbalanced datasets.

ROC Curve & AUC

OK
"The probability that a randomly chosen positive ranks higher than a randomly chosen negative."
Formula
\(\text{TPR} = \frac{TP}{TP + FN}\)   \(\text{FPR} = \frac{FP}{FP + TN}\)

\(\text{AUC} = \int_0^1 \text{TPR}(t)\, d(\text{FPR}(t))\)
Layman Analogy
Imagine ranking all customers by risk score. ROC shows how good your ranking is. AUC of 0.5 = coin flip, 1.0 = perfect separation.
Live AUC
—
Red Flag AUC below 0.7 is weak. Below 0.6 means your model barely beats random chance.
Pro Tip ROC-AUC is threshold-independent — it evaluates ranking quality, not a specific decision point.

PR Curve & Average Precision

OK
"When positives are rare, PR-AUC zooms in on how well you handle the rare class."
Formula
\(\text{AP} = \sum_n (R_n - R_{n-1}) \cdot P_n\)

Average Precision is the weighted mean of precisions at each recall step.
Layman Analogy
When positives are rare (like fraud at 0.1%), ROC-AUC can look great even for bad models. PR-AUC is the honest report card for imbalanced data.
Live AP
—
Red Flag PR-AUC below 0.5 means the model struggles to find positives without drowning in false positives.
Pro Tip Compare PR-AUC to the prevalence baseline. If your AP barely exceeds the positive rate, the model adds little value.

Cohen's Kappa

OK
"How much better are you than a monkey throwing darts? Kappa removes the luck factor from accuracy."
Formula
\(\kappa = \frac{p_o - p_e}{1 - p_e}\)

\(p_o\) = observed agreement
\(p_e\) = expected (chance) agreement
Interpretation Scale
<0.0 = Poor  |  0.0–0.20 = Slight
0.21–0.40 = Fair  |  0.41–0.60 = Moderate
0.61–0.80 = Substantial  |  0.81–1.0 = Almost Perfect

Layman Analogy

Decile / Score Band Analysis

OK
"Divide your predictions into 10 equal buckets from lowest to highest risk. The event rate should increase monotonically — if decile 5 has MORE events than decile 6, your model's ranking is broken in that region."
How It Works
Sort all predictions by score, divide into 10 equal-sized buckets (deciles). Compute event rate per bucket. A good model shows monotonically increasing event rates from decile 1 (lowest risk) to decile 10 (highest risk).
Layman Analogy
Imagine sorting loan applicants by risk score into 10 groups. The riskiest group should have the most defaults. If a middle group suddenly has more defaults than a higher-risk group, your model is confused in that region.

OK

Layman Analogy

1.0
